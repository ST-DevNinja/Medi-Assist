Abdominal pain refers to discomfort or pain that occurs in the region between the chest and pelvic area. It is one of the most common medical complaints and can range from mild and intermittent to severe and life-threatening. Abdominal pain may be generalized (affecting more than half the abdomen), localized (confined to one area), cramp-like, or colicky in nature.

**Common symptoms associated with abdominal pain include**:
- Sharp or stabbing pain
- Cramping or spasms
- Bloating or swelling
- Gas and flatulence
- Tenderness to touch
- Nausea and vomiting
- Diarrhea or constipation
- Loss of appetite
- Fever in case of infection
- Pain that worsens after eating or during movement

**Possible causes of abdominal pain** range from benign to serious and include:
- Indigestion (dyspepsia)
- Gas buildup or bloating
- Constipation
- Gastritis or acid reflux (GERD)
- Food poisoning or gastrointestinal infection
- Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)
- Peptic ulcers
- Appendicitis (inflammation of the appendix, often causing sharp pain in the lower right abdomen)
- Gallstones (cholelithiasis)
- Kidney stones
- Urinary tract infection (UTI)
- Crohnâ€™s disease or ulcerative colitis
- Pancreatitis (inflammation of the pancreas)
- Ectopic pregnancy or ovarian cysts (in women)
- Hernias
- Intestinal obstruction or volvulus

**Diagnosis** involves a thorough clinical evaluation including:
- Detailed patient history (location, duration, and nature of pain)
- Physical examination (e.g., rebound tenderness)
- Blood and urine tests to check for infection or inflammation
- Imaging studies such as abdominal ultrasound, X-ray, or CT scan
- Endoscopy or colonoscopy if gastrointestinal issues are suspected

**Treatment options** depend on the underlying cause:
- Dietary modifications (low-fat or low-FODMAP diet)
- Over-the-counter medications like antacids, antispasmodics, or laxatives
- Antibiotics for bacterial infections
- Rehydration for diarrhea or vomiting
- Pain relievers (with caution)
- Surgery in emergencies such as appendicitis, perforated ulcer, or intestinal blockage

**When to seek immediate medical attention**:
- Severe, sudden onset pain
- Pain with high fever
- Blood in vomit or stool
- Inability to pass stool or gas
- Persistent nausea and vomiting
- Swelling or distension of the abdomen
- Pain during pregnancy

**Synonyms and related expressions**:
- Stomach pain
- Belly ache
- Gastric pain
- Tummy ache
- Lower abdominal cramping
- Epigastric discomfort
- Gut pain
- Pelvic or flank pain
- Abdominal tenderness
- Burning in the stomach

Timely identification and management of the underlying cause of abdominal pain are crucial to prevent complications and ensure recovery. Preventative strategies include maintaining a balanced diet, drinking sufficient water, managing stress, and avoiding overeating or trigger foods.
